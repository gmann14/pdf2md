- Familiar symbols or designs

- [Mere variations of typographic ornamentation, lettering, or coloring](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ16a.pdf)

- Mere listings of ingredients or contents

Who Can Claim Copyright?

[The copyright in a work initially belongs to the author(s) who created that work. When two or more](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) authors create a single work with the intent of merging their contributions into inseparable or inter - dependent parts of a unitary whole, the authors are considered joint authors and have an indivisible [interest in the work as a whole. By contrast, if multiple authors contribute to a collective work, each](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38a.pdf)

author’s individual contribution is separate and distinct from the copyright ownership in the collec - tive work as a whole. “Works made for hire” are an important exception to the general rule for claiming copyright. When a work is made for hire, the author is not the individual who actually created the work. [Instead, the party that hired the individual is considered the author and the copyright owner of the](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/fees.html) [work. Whether a work is made for hire is determined by the facts that exist at the time the work is](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/fees.html) [created. There are two situations in which a work may be made for hire:](http://www.copyright.gov/)

[1.](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf) [When the work is created by an employee as part of the employee’s regular duties, or](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf)

2. [When an individual and the hiring party enter into an express written agreement that the](http://www.copyright.gov/) work is to be considered a “work made for hire” and the work is specially ordered or commis - [sioned for use as:](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf)

- A compilation

- [•](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) [A contribution to a collective work](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf)

- [•](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07b.pdf) [A part of a motion picture or other audiovisual work](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07b.pdf)

- A translation

- A supplementary work

- An instructional text

- A test

- Answer material for a test

- An atlas

The concept of work made for hire can be complicated and has serious consequences for both the [individual who creates the work and the hiring party who is considered to be the author and copy](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ30.pdf) - [right owner of the work. Transfer of Copyright Ownership

[Any or all of the copyright owner’s exclusive rights, or parts of those rights, can be transferred. The](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap1900/ch1900-publication.pdf) [transfer, however, generally must be made in writing and signed by the owner of the rights conveyed](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ16a.pdf) [or the owner’s authorized agent. Transferring a right on a nonexclusive basis does not require a writ](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ16a.pdf) - ten agreement. [You can bequeath a copyright by will or pass it along as personal property under applicable state](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ33.pdf) laws of intestate succession. It can also be conveyed by operation of law. You can “record” a transfer of copyright ownership with the Copyright Office through its Office [of Public Records and Repositories. Although recordation is not required to make a valid transfer](https://www.copyright.gov/fair-use/) [between parties, it does provide certain legal advantages. For more information, see](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) Recordation of Transfers and Other Documents [(](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) [Circular 12](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) [).](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf)

Termination of a Copyright Transfer

Under certain circumstances, the Copyright Act allows authors or their heirs to terminate an agree - ment that transferred or licensed the author’s copyright to a third party after thirty-five years. To terminate a grant, the author or the author’s heirs must serve an advance written “notice of termi - [nation” on the grantee or the grantee’s successor-in-interest and must record a copy of that notice](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/fees.html) [with the Copyright Office and pay the required](http://www.copyright.gov/) [filing fee](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/fees.html) [.](http://www.copyright.gov/) [A notice of termination must be recorded before the effective date of termination specified in the](http://www.copyright.gov/) notice. If a notice of termination is not recorded in a timely manner, the notice will be invalid, and [the author or the author’s heirs will not be able to terminate the agreement. For more information,](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf) see [chapter 2300](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf) , section 2310 of the [Compendium of U.S. Copyright Office Practices](http://www.copyright.gov/) .

[How Long Does Copyright Last?](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf)

[In general, for works created on or after January 1, 1978, the term of copyright is the life of the](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) [author plus seventy years after the author’s death. If the work is a joint work with multiple authors,](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) [the term lasts for seventy years after the last surviving author’s death. For works made for hire and](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07b.pdf) anonymous or pseudonymous works, the duration of copyright is 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter. For works created before January 1, 1978, that were not published or registered as of that date, the term of copyright is generally the same as for works created on or after January 1, 1978. The law, however, provides that in no case would the term have expired before December 31, 2002, and if the work was published on or before that date, the term will not expire before December 31, 2047. For works created before January 1, 1978, that were published or registered before that date, the initial term of copyright was twenty-eight years from the date of publication with notice or from the date of registration. At the end of the initial term, the copyright could be renewed for another sixty- [seven years for a total term of protection of up to ninety-five years. To extend copyright into the](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ30.pdf) [renewal term, two registrations had to be made before the original term expired: one for the original](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ30.pdf) term and the other for the renewal term. This requirement was eliminated on June 26, 1992, and renewal term registration is now optional. [For more information on the term of copyright protection, see](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ06a.pdf) Duration of Copyright [(](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ15a.pdf) [Circular](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ15a.pdf) [15A](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ15a.pdf) ) and Renewal of Copyright [(](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ06a.pdf) [Circular 6](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ06a.pdf) [A](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ06a.pdf) ).

How Can I Protect My Work?

[Copyright exists automatically in an original work of authorship once it is fixed in a tangible](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ33.pdf) [medium, but a copyright owner can take steps to enhance the protections of copyright, the most](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ33.pdf) important of which is registering the work. Although registering a work is not mandatory, for U.S. works, registration (or refusal) is necessary to enforce the exclusive rights of copyright through litigation. [Applying a copyright notice to a work has not been required since March 1, 1989, but may still](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) [provide practical and legal benefits. Notice typically consists of the copyright symbol or the word](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) “Copyright,” the name of the copyright owner, and the year of first publication. Placing a copyright notice on a work is not a substitute for registration.

Benefits of Registration

Registration establishes a claim to copyright with the Copyright Office. An application for copyright registration can be filed by the author or owner of an exclusive right in a work, the owner of all exclusive rights, or an agent on behalf of an author or owner. An application contains three essential [elements: a completed application form, a nonrefundable filing fee, and a nonreturnable deposit—](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/fees.html) [that is, a copy or copies of the work being registered and “deposited” with the Copyright Office.](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/fees.html) [A certificate of registration creates a public record of key facts relating to the authorship and owner](http://www.copyright.gov/) - ship of the claimed work, including the title of the work, the author of the work, the name and [address of the claimant or copyright owner, the year of creation, and information about whether](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf) [the work is published, has been previously registered, or includes preexisting material.](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf) You can submit an application online through [www.copyright.gov](http://www.copyright.gov/) or on a paper application. [For more information on registering a work with the Copyright Office, see](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf) Copyright Registration [(](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf) [Circular 2](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf) [).](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf) In addition to establishing a public record of a copyright claim, registration offers several other [statutory advantages:](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf)

- [Before an infringement suit may be filed in court, registration (or refusal) is necessary for](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07b.pdf) U.S. works.

- Registration establishes prima facie evidence of the validity of the copyright and facts stated in the certificate when registration is made before or within five years of publication.

- When registration is made prior to infringement or within three months after publication of a work, a copyright owner is eligible for statutory damages, attorneys’ fees, and costs.

- Registration permits a copyright owner to establish a record with the U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) for protection against the importation of infringing copies.

Registration can be made at any time within the life of the copyright. If you register before pub - [lication, you do not have to re-register when the work is published, although you can register the](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ30.pdf) published edition, if desired.

Effective Date of Registration

[When the Copyright Office registers a work it assigns an effective date of registration to the certifi](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ15a.pdf) - [cate of registration. The effective date of registration is the day that the Office receives in proper](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ06a.pdf) [form all required elements—an acceptable application, an acceptable deposit, and a nonrefundable](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap1900/ch1900-publication.pdf) [filing fee. The date is not set until all the required elements are in the Office’s possession. If the](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap1900/ch1900-publication.pdf) [Office receives incomplete materials, an unacceptable deposit, or an insufficient fee, the effective](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ16a.pdf) [date of registration will be set on the date that the Office receives all the required materials in](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ16a.pdf) [acceptable form. The date is not based on how long it takes the Office to examine the materials or](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ33.pdf) mail the certificate of registration. You do not have to receive your certificate of registration before you publish or produce your work. Nor do you need permission from the Copyright Office to place a copyright notice on your [work. But the Copyright Office must approve or refuse your application before you can file a lawsuit](https://www.copyright.gov/fair-use/) [for copyright infringement, except in cases involving a non-U.S. work.](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) You may seek statutory dam - [ages and attorneys’ fees in an infringement action provided that the infringement began](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) after the effective date of registration. The law, however, provides a grace period of three months after pub - lication during which full remedies can be recovered for any infringement begun during the three [months after publication if registration is made before this period ends.](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38a.pdf)

Copyright Notice

A copyright notice is a statement placed on copies or phonorecords of a work to inform the public that a copyright owner is claiming ownership of the work. A copyright notice consists of three elements:

- The copyright symbol © or (p) for phonorecords, the word “Copyright,” or the abbreviation “Copr.”;

- [The year of first publication of the work (or of creation if the work is unpublished); and](http://www.copyright.gov/)

- The name of the copyright owner, an abbreviation by which the name can be recognized, or a [generally known alternative designation.](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf) [A notice should be affixed to copies or phonorecords of a work in a way that gives reasonable](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf) notice of the claim of copyright. [Using a copyright notice is optional for unpublished works, non-U.S. works, and works published](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf) [on or after March 1, 1989. However, notice conveys the following benefits:](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf)

- It puts potential users on notice that copyright is claimed in the work.

- [•](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) [For published works, notice may prevent a defendant from attempting to limit liability for](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) [damages or injunctive relief based on an “innocent infringement” defense.](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07b.pdf)

- It identifies the copyright owner at the time of first publication for parties seeking permission to use the work.

- It identifies the year of first publication, which can be used to determine the term of copyright for anonymous or pseudonymous works or works made for hire.

- It may prevent the work from becoming an “orphan” by identifying the copyright owner or specifying the term of copyright. Orphan works are original works of authorship for which prospective users cannot identify or locate copyright owners to request permission.

Notice was required for works published in the United States before March 1, 1989. Works pub - [lished without notice before that date may have entered the public domain in this country.](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ30.pdf) For more information, see Copyright Notice ( [Circular 3](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ03.pdf) ).

[How Can I Use a Copyrighted Work?](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ06a.pdf)

[When deciding to use a work protected by copyright, the general rule is to seek permission from the](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap1900/ch1900-publication.pdf) [copyright owner. Under the copyright law, a copyright owner may authorize activities that fall under](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap1900/ch1900-publication.pdf) [the exclusive rights of copyright. For more information on seeking permission to use a copyrighted](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ16a.pdf) work, see How to Obtain Permission [(](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ16a.pdf) [Circular 16](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ16a.pdf) A ). [Sections 107 to 122 of the copyright law contain provisions that establish limitations on the exclu](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ33.pdf) - [sive rights of the copyright owner. The provisions make certain uses of copyrighted works permis](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ33.pdf) - sible without first obtaining permission of the copyright owner. One of the most discussed of these statutory provisions is known as fair use, a legal doctrine that promotes freedom of expression by [permitting the unlicensed use of copyright-protected works in certain circumstances. For more](https://www.copyright.gov/fair-use/) [information on fair use, see the Office’s](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) [Fair Use Index](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) on its website.

What Is Publication and Why Is It Important?

Under copyright law, publication is the distribution of copies or phonorecords of a work to the public by sale or other transfer of ownership or by rental, lease, or lending. Offering to distribute [copies or phonorecords to a group of people for purposes of further distribution, public perfor](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/fees.html) - [mance, or public display also constitutes publication.](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/fees.html) [Whether a work is published has important implications, including:](http://www.copyright.gov/)

- The year of publication may determine the length of the copyright term for a work made for [hire or an anonymous or pseudonymous work.](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf)

- [The year of publication may determine the length of the copyright term if the work was cre](http://www.copyright.gov/) - ated before January 1, 1978, and was published or registered before that date.

- [•](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf) [The year of publication may determine the length of the copyright term if the work was created](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf) before January 1, 1978, and was first published between January 1, 1978, and December 31, 2002.

- [•](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) [The date and nation of first publication may determine if a non-U.S. work is eligible for copy](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) - [right protection in the United States.](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf)

- A certificate of registration creates certain legal presumptions if the work is registered before or within five years after the work was first published.

- A copyright owner may be entitled to claim statutory damages and attorneys’ fees in an infringement lawsuit if the work was registered before the infringement began or within three months after the first publication of that work.

- Many of the exceptions and limitations on the copyright owner’s exclusive rights vary depend - ing on whether the work is published or unpublished.

- As a general rule, works published before March 1, 1989, must be published with a valid copy - right notice.

- [The deposit requirements for registering a published work differ from the requirements for](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ30.pdf) registering an unpublished work.

- [Works published in the United States may be subject to mandatory deposit with the Library of](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ15a.pdf) [Congress. For more information, see “What Is Mandatory Deposit?”](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ06a.pdf) below.

[When you register your work with the Office, you must determine whether the work is published](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap1900/ch1900-publication.pdf) or unpublished. For further information regarding publication, see [chapter 1900](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap1900/ch1900-publication.pdf) of Compendium of U.S. Copyright Office Practices .

How Do I Protect My Work in Other Countries?

There is no such thing as an “international copyright” that automatically protects an author’s works [throughout the entire world. Protection against unauthorized use in a particular country depends](https://www.copyright.gov/fair-use/) [on the national laws of that country. Most countries offer protection to non-U.S. works under](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) [certain conditions, and these conditions have been greatly simplified by international copyright](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ12.pdf) treaties and conventions. Generally, a U.S. work may be protected in another country if that country has entered into an international agreement with the United States. For more information and a list [of countries that maintain copyright relations with the United States, see](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38a.pdf) International Copyright Relations of the United States [(](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38a.pdf) [Circular 38A](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38a.pdf) [).](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38a.pdf)

What Is Mandatory Deposit?

All copyrighted works that are published in the United States are subject to the “mandatory deposit” provision of the copyright law. As a general rule, this provision requires that two complete copies of [the “best edition” of a copyrightable work published in the United States be sent to the Copyright](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/fees.html) [Office for the collections of the Library of Congress within three months of publication. The “best](https://www.copyright.gov/docs/fees.html) [edition” of a work is “the edition, published in the United States at any time before the date of](http://www.copyright.gov/) deposit, that the Library of Congress determines to be most suitable for its purposes.” The owner of [copyright or of the exclusive right of publication may comply with this requirement either by sub](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf) - [mitting the best edition of the work when registering the work with the Office or by submitting the](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/chap2300/ch2300-recordation.pdf) [work without seeking a registration and solely for the purpose of fulfilling the mandatory deposit](http://www.copyright.gov/) [requirement. The mandatory deposit provision helps ensure that the Library of Congress obtains](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf) [copies of every copyrightable work published in the United States for its collections or for exchange](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ02.pdf) with or transfer to any other library. [For more information, see](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) Mandatory Deposit of Copies or Phonorecords for the Library of Congress [(](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) [Circular 7D](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) [) and](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07d.pdf) Best Edition of Published Copyrighted Works for the Collections of the Library of Con - gress [(](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07b.pdf) [Circular 7B](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07b.pdf) [).](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ07b.pdf)

Upon](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ30.pdf) [registration, the copyright recordation will continue to receive the benefits of border enforcement](http://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ30.pdf) from CBP. 5.
